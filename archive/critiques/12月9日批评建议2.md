GRA-CNN Manuscript Pre-Submission Review and Improvement Plan
1. Language and Logic Expression Evaluation (Section by Section)

Overall Clarity: The manuscript is generally well-written and organized, but there are a few verbose or unclear phrases and some places where the logical flow can be tightened. Below we evaluate each section and suggest improvements:

Title and Abstract

Title: The current title “GRA-CNN: A Novel Convolutional Neural Network Method with Gray Relational Analysis” is understandable but could be more specific. It doesn’t explicitly mention pruning, which is the paper’s focus. We suggest making the title more precise about structured pruning. For example: “GRA-CNN: Structured Channel Pruning of CNNs via Gray Relational Analysis.” This highlights the pruning aspect and the method (GRA) more clearly.

Abstract: The abstract covers context, the proposed method, and key results, which is good. However, a few sentences are lengthy. For instance, “Structured pruning addresses this by removing redundant filters, but existing criteria typically rely on local statistics… ignoring the global semantic alignment…” can be split for clarity. Ensure the abstract emphasizes the unique contribution (using GRA for semantic alignment) in a concise way. Also, double-check for minor typos introduced by formatting (e.g., remove hyphenation artifacts like “suc‐cess” in the PDF).

Suggestion: Simplify and break up long sentences in the abstract. Emphasize how GRA-CNN differs from existing methods and include the most striking result. For example, mention the 50% compression with negligible accuracy loss more explicitly.

Prompt (Abstract Refinement): “Rewrite the abstract to be clearer and more concise, highlighting the novelty (Gray Relational Analysis for pruning) and the strongest experimental results (e.g. 50% FLOPs reduction with minimal accuracy drop).”

Introduction

Clarity: The introduction is well-structured, but there are a few instances of informal wording. For example, “heavy heuristics” in “they often introduce additional optimization loops, hyperparameters, and heavy heuristics” could be replaced with a more academic phrase like “numerous ad-hoc hyperparameters and heuristics.” Another example is “visually energetic channel” – this phrase is a bit vague. It might be clearer to say “a channel with high activation magnitude”, since “energetic” is not a standard term in this context.

Logic and Flow: The logical progression is strong: it moves from limitations of existing pruning criteria to the need for a semantic metric. One small gap is the reference to “relation-graph pruning [6,7]” where reference [7] is actually a survey, not a specific method. To avoid confusion, you might cite a concrete method instead of or in addition to the survey for relation-graph approaches. Overall, though, the motivations are clearly stated.

Contribution bullets: The bullet list of contributions is helpful. Just ensure consistency in tone: each bullet should start with a verb in the same form. Currently, they do (“We introduce…”, “We design…”, “We show…”, “We provide…” – this is good). One grammar note: “multi‐ple architectures (ResNet-20/56/110, ResNet-18, VGG-16)” – make sure “multiple” isn’t split by a hyphen in the final text.

Terminology: There is a minor terminology issue in the intro’s last paragraph of related work: “logic outputs” in “quantify the semantic alignment between feature maps and logic outputs”. This seems to be a typo and should be “logit outputs” or just “logits”, since you compare feature maps to class logits. Correcting this will prevent misunderstanding.

Prompt (Introduction Clarity): “Improve the introduction’s clarity and academic tone. For example, replace informal phrases like ‘heavy heuristics’ with formal language, and ensure technical terms are correct (e.g., use ‘logits’ instead of ‘logic outputs’). Preserve the logical flow and emphasis on the gap in existing work that GRA-CNN addresses.”

Experiments and Discussion (Sections 4 & 5)

Section 4 Organization: The experimental evaluation is very comprehensive, covering CIFAR-10 (with multiple networks), Tiny-ImageNet, a deeper ResNet-110, VGG-16, inference efficiency, statistical reliability, and ablation. The subsections are logically ordered for the most part. One observation: Section 4.4 vs 4.3: 4.3 shows Tiny-ImageNet results, and 4.4 covers “deeper networks and the more challenging CIFAR-100 dataset”. This is fine, but a reader might wonder why CIFAR-100 results were not a standalone subsection. You might consider renaming 4.4 to reflect both aspects (e.g., “Scalability: Deeper Network (ResNet-110) and Harder Dataset (CIFAR-100)”). In the text, make a clear transition like “Next, we evaluate the scalability of GRA-CNN to a deeper architecture and a more fine-grained dataset.”

Result Description: The comparisons are generally described well with references to figures and tables. A couple of places could use smoother transitions:

After 4.2 (alternative metrics comparison), you jump to 4.3 Tiny-ImageNet. It would help to introduce 4.3 with a sentence like “Having validated GRA on CIFAR-10, we next test its performance on a larger dataset (Tiny-ImageNet) to assess scalability to more complex data.”

Section 4.5 (Inference Efficiency) should explicitly mention the hardware or environment used for timing (if not, please add this). Currently, the table shows speedups (e.g., 1.06×, 1.66×) but the text should say something like “measured on an Intel CPU” or whatever platform, so that readers know these are real-wall-clock improvements, not just theoretical FLOPs.

Fine-tuning and Implementation Details: One notable omission is detail on training/fine-tuning parameters. The paper should mention the fine-tuning process: e.g., “We fine-tune pruned models for XX epochs on each dataset with an initial learning rate YY (decayed by …), using SGD/Adam with momentum …” etc. Currently, such details are not found in the text (e.g., no mention of learning rate or epochs was found). Adding a short “Implementation Details” paragraph in Section 4.1 or a table note will improve reproducibility. For example: describe dataset train/test splits, baseline model accuracies, and any hyperparameters for pruning (like the default ρ value used, which is presumably 0.5 unless otherwise noted).

Discussion (Section 5): The manuscript includes a brief discussion after the experiments, with subheadings “Limitations”, “Potential extensions”, and “Takeaway”. This is a good approach to discuss the broader impact. Ensure these appear clearly as sub-sections or emphasized paragraphs. We have a couple of suggestions here:

Limitations: The points noted (dependence on stable logits, batch-level analysis only) are important. You might add one more limitation for honesty: e.g., mention that ImageNet-scale evaluation was not performed. Reviewers might note the experiments did not include ImageNet; pre-empt this by stating it and perhaps arguing that Tiny-ImageNet was used as a proxy due to resource constraints.

Potential Extensions: This is forward-looking and great. The list of applying semantic relational scoring to transformers, MLPs, etc., is insightful. Just ensure the formatting is consistent (it looks like bullet points in the PDF). Perhaps use an ordered list if these are separate ideas, or at least a clear bulleted list. Also, consider citing a relevant reference for each idea if available (you did cite [25,26] for transformers pruning which is good).

Takeaway: The single-sentence takeaway is strong: “Structured pruning should not only remove redundant structure but preserve essential semantics.” This nicely encapsulates the philosophy. You might italicize or bold Semantic preservation as a key phrase here for emphasis, since it’s the main message.

Grammar and Style: A few sentences in the experimental analysis are long. For example, “Our experiments across multiple datasets and architectures demonstrate that semantic alignment is a powerful signal for structured pruning: GRA-CNN preserves accuracy under aggressive compression, accelerates fine-tuning convergence, and captures importance information nearly orthogonal to classical criteria.”. This is actually in the Conclusion, but it reads like an experimental summary. It’s a good sentence, but consider splitting it into two for readability (one sentence on preserves accuracy and convergence, another on the orthogonal nature of the information captured).

Also, make sure each figure or table reference in the text is accompanied by an explanation. For instance, “(Figure 5 suggests GRA-CNN fine-tunes faster than L1)” is explained with two bullet points – that’s good. Just verify no figure is referenced without at least a brief discussion of what to observe in it.

Prompt (Editing Experiments Section): “Review the experiment section for any missing details and long sentences. Add any needed information (training hyperparameters, hardware used for inference measurements). Simplify complex sentences and ensure smooth transitions between subsections.”

Conclusion

The conclusion is concise and to the point. It restates the core idea (shifting from structural heuristics to semantic alignment) and summarizes the benefits demonstrated (accuracy under compression, faster convergence, complementary to classical criteria). This is a strong ending.

One suggestion is to add one forward-looking sentence. For example, you might end with a note like: “In future work, we plan to explore GRA-guided pruning on larger-scale tasks and different architectures (e.g., transformers), to further validate its generality.” This aligns with your “Potential extensions” discussion but placing a forward-looking statement in the conclusion itself can leave a positive impression.

Also, ensure the conclusion stands as a standalone section (it’s currently Section 6 in the PDF). It should not introduce new information, just synthesize. You’ve done that well. Just minor rephrasing: instead of “captures importance information nearly orthogonal to classical criteria”, you could say “captures an importance signal nearly orthogonal to that of classical criteria” for a slightly smoother phrasing.

Prompt (Conclusion Polish): “Polish the conclusion paragraph to reinforce the main contributions and results in a confident tone. Add a closing remark about future work or broader impact (e.g., generalization to other domains), while keeping it brief.”

4. Innovation and Journal Fit Analysis

Novelty: The core innovation of this paper is the introduction of Gray Relational Analysis (GRA) as a criterion for structured channel pruning. This is a fresh idea – to our knowledge, GRA has not been used for CNN pruning before. In the context of recent literature, most pruning criteria fall into magnitude, spatial redundancy, or information-based categories. Your method brings in a sequence similarity perspective, aligning internal features with final outputs. This semantic criterion is conceptually different and bridges a gap between pruning and interpretability.

Compared to recent works:

Cheng et al. (2022, Applied Intelligence) used global channel relationship graphs for pruning
dblp.org
, and Dong et al. (2023, Applied Intelligence) modeled closeness-centrality in feature maps for pruning
researchgate.net
. These approaches also recognize the importance of relationships beyond single-channel magnitude. However, GRA-CNN has a relative advantage in simplicity and focus: GRA directly measures the agreement of a channel’s behavior with the model’s decisions, which is a more direct semantic alignment than constructing graphs or computing complex centrality measures. This likely makes GRA-CNN easier to implement and possibly more general. Moreover, GRA-CNN doesn’t require additional training loops (as some reinforcement learning or NAS-based pruning methods do) – it’s a post-hoc criterion that can be computed efficiently.

Information-theoretic methods (MI-based) [15–19] attempt to preserve mutual information between features and outputs. Those are conceptually similar in goal (retain information relevant to outputs) but they often require estimating MI, which can be computationally heavy or need strong assumptions. GRA provides a lightweight alternative – essentially nonparametric and requiring just sorting of a relational grade. The experiments showed GRA achieves comparable results to MI, which is a strong point of novelty: a simpler metric matches a theoretically optimal (but expensive) criterion.

Dynamic or Automated pruning methods (like those using reinforcement learning, or ACPLI [14]) are a different line. They focus on how to search for which channels to prune, sometimes learning the pruning decision with gradients. GRA-CNN could actually complement those – for example, one could use GRA as a heuristic within a search-based method. But as a standalone, GRA-CNN’s novelty is focusing on what makes a channel important (semantic alignment) rather than on a new search strategy. This is a distinct angle.

Theoretical insight: The paper provides an intuitive mechanism (Section 3.3) for why GRA works, and an empirical correlation analysis showing it selects channels that magnitude-based methods would overlook. This contributes new understanding: it suggests there are “silent but important” channels (low magnitude yet critical for decisions) that GRA can identify, whereas L1 pruning would erroneously drop them. This insight is an innovation in thinking about model pruning – connecting it to concepts of trend consistency and decision relevance. Not many pruning papers have articulated this in the same way.
