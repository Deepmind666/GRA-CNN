\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Channel Pruning with Local Importance Criteria}

Early structured pruning methods evaluate channel importance using local statistics of weights or feature maps.
Magnitude-based criteria prune filters with small $\ell_1$ or $\ell_2$ norms~\cite{han2015deep,he2017channel}, while
Network Slimming~\cite{liu2017network_slimming} learns channel-wise sparsity on batch-normalization scaling factors
and removes channels with small scaling parameters.
Geometric criteria, such as FPGM~\cite{he2019fpgm}, instead identify redundant filters by measuring the distance
to a geometric median in weight space, and prune filters that are closest to the median.
Rank-based methods like HRank~\cite{lin2020hrank} analyze the rank of feature maps; filters that consistently produce
low-rank activations are regarded as less informative and are removed.
Although these approaches are simple and effective, the underlying signals are purely structural:
they measure local properties of weights or activations, but do not directly model how a channel contributes
to the final semantic decision of the network.

\subsection{Global Relation and Automatic Channel Pruning}

To go beyond local statistics, several works attempt to exploit global relations among channels or to automate the pruning pipeline.
Chang et al.~\cite{chang2022acp} propose ACP, which clusters channels and uses swarm intelligence optimization
to search for a good subset of channels to keep, effectively automating the pruning decision.
Other works construct global similarity graphs between channels or layers and prune those that are highly redundant
in the learned relation space~\cite{hu2023dynamic_pruning,li2023lightweight_dnn}.
These methods capture richer interactions than simple magnitude criteria, but the relation is still defined
\emph{within} the network (e.g., between channels or layers) and remains largely agnostic to the semantic output.
Moreover, clustering-- or search-based strategies often introduce many hyper-parameters and non-trivial computational overhead.

In parallel, there is a line of work on designing lightweight architectures from scratch, such as MobileNet and its variants,
or on searching compact networks via neural architecture search and pruning co-design~\cite{howard2017mobilenets,tan2019efficientnet}.
These methods are complementary to channel pruning: they provide efficient architectures,
while pruning focuses on compressing a given pretrained network.
Our method follows the latter paradigm but introduces a different importance signal that is orthogonal to
existing local or relation-based criteria.

\subsection{Gray Relational Analysis in Machine Learning}

Gray Relational Analysis (GRA) originates from gray system theory~\cite{deng1989gra}, and is widely used to measure
the similarity between temporal sequences in control, decision making, and economic forecasting.
Recent works have applied GRA to feature selection, multi-criteria decision analysis, and stock market prediction,
where the goal is to select variables that exhibit strong relational behavior with a target sequence.
However, to the best of our knowledge, GRA has not been systematically explored as a channel importance metric
for deep neural network pruning.

\subsection{Our Positioning}

Compared with existing pruning criteria, GRA-CNN introduces a \emph{semantic relational} view of channel importance.
Instead of judging a channel by its weight magnitude, spatial redundancy, or feature-map rank,
we treat each channel as a temporal sequence of activations over a mini-batch and measure its gray relational grade
with respect to the corresponding logit sequence of the ground-truth class.
This design directly encodes how closely the behavior of a channel aligns with the final decision of the network.
In other words, GRA-CNN is complementary to magnitude-, geometry-, and rank-based criteria:
it operates at the level of \emph{sequence-level semantic alignment}, rather than purely local statistics.
This unique perspective is what distinguishes our work from ACP~\cite{chang2022acp} and other global relation methods,
and our experiments show that combining such semantic relational scores with structured pruning
leads to competitive or superior performance under aggressive compression.
